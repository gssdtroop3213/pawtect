⚠️ Wildlife Risks in Our Area

Living near open space means wildlife is genuinely close by, not just occasionally. Coyotes, snakes, and other animals move through neighborhoods regularly, and knowing what to expect makes a real difference.

🐺 Coyotes

Coyotes are the biggest threat to dogs in communities near wild areas. They're smart, fast, and they've learned to live around people. Small dogs are most at risk, but coyotes have gone after medium-sized dogs too.

They're most active at dusk and dawn, but that's not the only time you'll see them. Spring is especially risky, because coyotes protecting pups are more aggressive than usual.

⚡ High-Risk Times
Coyote attacks on dogs spike in spring (pupping season, roughly March–June) and again when food is scarce in late summer and fall. Dawn and dusk walks carry extra risk year-round.

🐍 Snakes

Rattlesnakes are present in many Southern California communities, especially on rocky or sunny trails. They're more active in summer when they need warmth. Most snake encounters happen when a dog goes sniffing into bushes or off the trail.

💡 Trail tip
Keep your dog on-leash and on the path. Most snake bites on dogs happen when the dog wanders into tall grass or reaches into rocks.

🌿 Poison Oak

"Leaves of three, leave it be," but poison oak changes color by season. It's green in spring and summer (easy to miss), then red and orange in fall. Your dog can carry the oils home on their fur and transfer it to you without ever showing symptoms themselves.

🕷️ Ticks

Ticks are common in tall grass and brush. They can carry Lyme disease and other illnesses. After every trail walk, run your hands through your dog's fur, especially around the ears, neck, armpits, and between toes.

🛡️ Prevention Tips

Most encounters are preventable. These habits make a real difference.

Carry a bag on every walk

Always bring a plastic bag. If a coyote comes toward you, swing it through the air so it fills up and makes a loud whooshing sound. It sounds silly, but it works. Coyotes find the sudden movement and noise startling and will usually back off.

Watch your dog the entire time they're outside

Even a quick bathroom break in the backyard counts. Coyotes are fast and quiet. Don't assume your yard is safe just because it's fenced. Coyotes can clear a 6-foot fence.

⚡ Important
Never leave small dogs outside unsupervised, even for a few minutes. Coyotes have taken dogs from fenced yards and patios in San Diego communities.

Make yourself look big

If a coyote gets close: pick up your dog if they're small enough, raise your arms wide above your head, and use a loud, firm voice. You want to look like a threat, not prey. Most coyotes will retreat.

🚨 During a Wildlife Encounter

What you do in the first few seconds matters. Stay calm, your dog reads your energy. If you panic, they panic.

If the coyote is hunting

It probably isn't after you or your dog specifically. Still, don't wait to find out. Haze it immediately: loud voice, big arms, swing that bag. Give it an easy reason to leave. It will.

If the coyote is patrolling territory (spring)

Do NOT try to scare it off. A coyote protecting pups near a den is a different situation entirely. It's not going to be deterred the same way, and trying to haze it could push it toward aggression.

Instead: pull your dog in tight (or pick them up), keep walking at a calm, normal pace, and don't make direct eye contact. The coyote will stop following once you've moved past its territory boundary.

ℹ️ How to tell the difference
A hunting coyote moves purposefully and may be tracking something other than you. A territorial coyote will pace parallel to you, follow at a fixed distance, or position itself between you and a specific area (likely near a den). If it's doing the latter, don't push it. Just leave calmly.

If your dog tries to chase

Hold the leash firmly and don't let them get close. This is one of the most dangerous scenarios. Dogs that chase coyotes can get drawn into the pack. Don't let go of the leash for any reason.

❤️🩹 After a Wildlife Encounter

If your dog was attacked, the priority is getting them to a vet fast, even if the injury looks minor. Coyote bites can cause serious puncture wounds that aren't obvious under fur, and infection risk is high.

Right after the attack

  1. Apply gentle pressure to any bleeding wound with a clean cloth.
  2. Call the nearest emergency vet clinic before you arrive so they're ready.
  3. Get there as fast as you safely can.
⚡ Don't skip the vet
Even if your dog seems okay, go. Coyote bite wounds can be deeper than they look and carry a high infection risk. Rabies exposure also needs to be evaluated by a professional.

Life after the attack

About 50% of dogs show behavioral changes after a coyote attack. This is normal. Watch for:

  • Refusing to return to where the attack happened
  • Anxiety or fearfulness outdoors in general
  • Disrupted sleep or night terrors

These behaviors can get better with time and patience. One approach that helps: treat-based conditioning. Start by rewarding your dog for being comfortable near (but not at) the location of the attack. Gradually close the distance over days or weeks, always rewarding calm behavior. Don't rush it.
